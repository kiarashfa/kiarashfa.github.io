Research Works

Bridging Polymer Science and Artificial Intelligence

My research journey explores the intersection of polymer reaction engineering and machine learning.
Rather than choosing between data-driven approaches and first-principles models, I work to combine their strengths by incorporating domain knowledge from polymer chemistry into neural networks. This dual-tango between chemistry and AI has led to breakthroughs in predicting polymer properties, modeling complex polymerization processes, and even real-time control of chemical reactors.

Data-Driven Machine Learning

My journey began with pure data-driven approaches, training neural networks on experimental data to predict polymer properties that are difficult or time-consuming to measure. These foundational works demonstrated the power of machine learning in polymer science and highlighted both its potential and limitations.

An artificial neural network to predict reactivity ratios in radical copolymerization

Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Xabier Telleria-Allika, José M Asua, Nicholas Ballard

Developed an artificial neural network trained on 5,000+ monomer pairs that predicts reactivity ratios solely from molecular structure, significantly outperforming classical approaches like the Q-e scheme.

Year 2023
Journal Polymer Chemistry
Volume 14(23)
Publisher Royal Society of Chemistry
Graphical Abstract

Monomer reactivity ratios are central to understanding polymerization rate, copolymer composition, and sequence distribution, but their measurement is non-trivial. We developed an artificial neural network trained on 5,000+ monomer pairs from the Polymer Handbook that predicts reactivity ratios solely from molecular structure using fingerprints. The model significantly outperforms classical approaches like the Q-e scheme and enables predictions for monomer pairs with no available kinetic data.

Google Scholar DOI: 10.1039/D3PY00246B

Multi-property ML Models to Accelerate the Transition Towards Bio-Based Polymers

Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Nicholas Ballard

Created an integrated ML framework that simultaneously predicts four critical properties to systematically evaluate bio-based monomer pairs as replacements for conventional petroleum-based formulations.

Year 2026
Journal Advanced Intelligent Discovery
Volume 2(1)
Publisher John Wiley & Sons
Graphical Abstract

The transition toward sustainable polymers requires replacing petroleum-based monomers with bio-based alternatives. We present an integrated ML framework that simultaneously predicts four critical properties: propagation rate constant, reactivity ratios, glass transition temperature, and water solubility. Using neural networks and gradient boosting, we created a unified selection tool that systematically evaluates bio-based monomer pairs. Validated through experimental case studies replacing n-butyl acrylate/methyl methacrylate and n-butyl acrylate/styrene, our framework successfully identified viable bio-based alternatives achieving comparable performance to conventional systems.

Google Scholar DOI: 10.1002/aidi.70089

Informed Neural Networks

In polymer science and engineering, we don't have the luxury of abundant data. But even when we do have data, it would be a pity to use only the data while ignoring all the knowledge that the scientific community has developed over decades. This realization led us to pioneer polymer chemistry-informed neural networks (PCINNs). Inspired by physics-informed neural networks, this approach integrates domain knowledge from polymerization kinetics directly into the neural network training process. PCINNs combine the best of both worlds: the flexibility of machine learning with the reliability of first-principles chemistry, enabling us to build smarter models that respect the underlying science.

Polymer chemistry informed neural networks (PCINNs) for modelling of polymerization processes

Nicholas Ballard, Jon Larrañaga, Kiarash Farajzadehahary, José M Asua

Introduced a method to incorporate polymerization kinetic knowledge into neural network training, enabling accurate models using datasets as small as a single sample while maintaining extrapolation capabilities.

Year 2024
Journal Polymer Chemistry
Volume 15(44)
Publisher Royal Society of Chemistry
Graphical Abstract

Neural networks typically require large datasets and struggle with extrapolation. We demonstrate how polymerization kinetic models can be incorporated into the training process to overcome these limitations. This enables accurate, data-driven predictive models using datasets as small as a single sample. Demonstrated for solution polymerization, the approach significantly outperforms conventional neural networks and can even improve upon existing first-principles kinetic models.

Google Scholar DOI: 10.1039/D4PY00995A

PCINN for Predictive Models of Complex Emulsion Polymerization Processes

Shaghayegh Hamzehlou, Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Jon Larrañaga, Nicholas Ballard, José M. Asua

Extended PCINNs to the challenging case of emulsion polymerization, demonstrating substantially improved predictions for this complex industrial process and enabling fast, real-time predictions as a real-time on-the-fly model.

Year 2026
Journal Polymer Chemistry
Volume TBA(#)
Publisher Royal Society of Chemistry
Graphical Abstract

Emulsion polymerization modeling is exceptionally challenging, requiring understanding of compartmentalized radical polymerization kinetics and complex colloidal phenomena. We developed a PCINN that combines first-principles knowledge with data-driven components for seeded semibatch emulsion polymerization of n-butyl acrylate. Compared to purely mechanistic models and standard neural networks, PCINNs showed substantially improved prediction accuracy and can serve as on-the-fly models for fast, real-time predictions of this complex industrial process.

Reinforcement Learning

Beyond prediction, I work to integrate machine learning directly into the chemical environment by creating AI agents that can control polymerization reactors in real-time. This "dual-tango" between the virtual and physical worlds represents the cutting edge of process control, where reinforcement learning agents learn optimal control strategies through interaction with reactor systems.

Reinforcement learning for the optimization and online control of polymerization reactors: Particle morphology

Nicholas Ballard, Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Shaghayegh Hamzehlou, Usue Mori, José M Asua

Demonstrated that reinforcement learning can train neural networks to select optimal real-time control actions for targeting desired polymer particle morphology, with a state estimator enabling full online control.

Year 2024
Journal Computers & Chemical Engineering
Volume 187
Publisher Elsevier
Graphical Abstract

Online control of emulsion polymerization is challenging due to process complexity and difficulties in online measurement. We explored reinforcement learning for online control of particle morphology, showing in silico that given a mathematical model of system dynamics RL can train neural networks to select optimal real-time control actions targeting desired polymer products. Additionally, a second neural network serves as a state estimator, enabling full online control of particle morphology which cannot be directly measured online.

Google Scholar DOI: 10.1016/j.compchemeng.2024.108739

Reinforcement learning for the optimization and online control of polymerization reactors: Molar mass distributions

Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Shaghayegh Hamzehlou, Nicholas Ballard, José M. Asua

Developed an RL-based control system using a surrogate neural network model to enable real-time optimization of molar mass distribution for non-linear polymers, a previously intractable control problem.

Year 2026
Journal Computers & Chemical Engineering
Volume TBA(#)
Publisher Elsevier
Graphical Abstract

Controlling molar mass distribution (MMD) of non-linear polymers containing crosslinked/branched chains is notoriously difficult due to measurement limitations and computational complexity of kinetic models. We developed an RL-based control system for semi-batch emulsion polymerization of butyl acrylate that optimizes seed selection, feeding rates, and temperature while handling disturbances like pump errors. A key innovation is using a surrogate neural network-based model that dramatically reduces training time and provides predictive capabilities for operators to evaluate strategies and anticipate outcomes in real-time.

Supporting Work & Foundations

Behind every machine learning breakthrough lies robust foundational work. Developing accurate PCINNs and RL controllers for emulsion polymerization required first building reliable mechanistic models of the process itself. The work on the average number of radicals per particle (n̄) not only solved a long-standing measurement problem but also provided the modeling foundation that made our subsequent PCINN and RL work possible. Meanwhile, our comprehensive review article in Progress in Polymer Science synthesizes the broader landscape of machine learning in polymer reaction engineering, contextualizing where this field has been and where it's headed.

The hidden secrets of the average number of radicals per particle (n̄)

Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Shaghayegh Hamzehlou, Nicholas Ballard, José M Asua

Showed that n̄ contains sufficient information to determine radical distribution and develop a soft sensor for online MMD estimation, enabling for the first time online control of MMD for non-linear emulsion polymers.

Year 2024
Journal Chemical Engineering Journal
Volume 487
Publisher Elsevier
Graphical Abstract

Emulsion polymerization is susceptible to run-to-run variations due to inability to measure key characteristics online, particularly MMD of non-linear polymers. We showed that the average number of radicals per particle (n̄), easily obtained from calorimetric measurements, contains sufficient information to determine the distribution of particles with n radicals. This knowledge enables accurate estimation of the effective termination rate coefficient and development of a soft sensor for on-line MMD estimation, offering for the first time the possibility of online control of MMD for non-linear emulsion polymers.

Google Scholar DOI: 10.1016/j.cej.2024.150681

Adding Machine Learning to the Polymer Reaction Engineering Toolbox

Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Shaghayegh Hamzehlou, Nicholas Ballard

A comprehensive review of machine learning advances in polymer reaction engineering, with focus on combining first-principles and data-driven approaches, published in the field's most prestigious journal.

Year 2025
Journal Progress in Polymer Science
Volume 170
Publisher Elsevier
Graphical Abstract

Mathematical modeling has long been crucial for designing polymeric materials. While traditionally grounded in first-principles, data-driven machine learning approaches have gained significant traction. This review discusses recent advances in applying ML to polymer reaction engineering, with particular focus on combining the strengths of both first-principles and data-driven models. We overview key ML techniques, explore successful applications in polymeric systems, present the state-of-the-art, and provide perspective on future challenges and opportunities in this nascent field.

Alkali-Soluble Resins as pH-Responsive Protective Colloids

Mehdi Naderi, Kiarash Farajzadehahary, Timo Melchin, Hans-Peter Weitzel, Jose R Leiza, José M Asua

Investigated pH-switchable alkali-soluble resins as stabilizers for emulsion polymers, enabling controlled coagulation for dry polymer isolation and subsequent restabilization in water.

Year 2025
Journal ACS Applied Materials & Interfaces
Volume 17(30), pp. 43750-43760
Publisher American Chemical Society
Graphical Abstract

Emulsion polymers are widely used in adhesives, coatings, and construction materials, often favored in dry form for easier storage and transportation. We investigated pH-switchable alkali-soluble resins as stabilizers that enable latex coagulation for polymer isolation, then restabilization in water. Low Tg vinyl acetate/vinyl neodecanoate particles stabilized by high Tg alkali-soluble resins were synthesized and successfully isolated through pH-controlled coagulation, drying, and grinding. The isolated polymer was then restabilized by adjusting pH and applying energy, demonstrating a practical route for switchable emulsion polymer systems.

Google Scholar DOI: 10.1021/acsami.5c12131
